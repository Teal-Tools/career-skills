# Reference: Nonviolent Communication (NVC)

Use this reference when a skill needs to help the user say a hard thing well: giving
feedback, raising a conflict, naming a pattern that keeps repeating, or preparing for
a conversation they have been avoiding.

The method comes from Marshall Rosenberg's **Nonviolent Communication**. The name is
unfortunate for workplace use — it sounds soft, and it is not. The point is not to be
gentle. The point is to say the true thing in a form the other person can actually
hear, instead of a form that makes them defend themselves.

**Recommended reading:** Marshall B. Rosenberg, *Nonviolent Communication: A Language
of Life* (PuddleDancer Press). Worth reading in full if the user does this often.

## The four steps

Every hard message decomposes into four parts. Most bad feedback fails because it
collapses them — usually by fusing an observation with a judgment, or by delivering a
complaint with no request attached.

| Step | What it answers | Sentence stem |
|------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Observation** | What is true | "When I saw / heard / noticed…" |
| **Feeling** | What is true *for me* | "I felt…" |
| **Need** | Why it landed that way | "Because I value / rely on…" |
| **Request** | What I want now | "Would you be willing to…?" |

An optional fifth step — **owning your part** — sits between Need and Request when
the user contributed to the situation. It is not throat-clearing; it is what makes
the message land as shared rather than accusatory.

The steps are a *thinking tool*, not a script. See "Don't sound like NVC" below.

## Step 1 — Observation, not story

This is the step that does the most work, and the one most people skip.

**The test: could a video camera have recorded it?** A camera records words, actions,
dates, and counts. It does not record intent, attitude, motive, or pattern-labels.

| Story (what you concluded) | Observation (what a camera saw) |
|---|---|
| "You don't care about quality." | "The last three PRs went to review with failing tests." |
| "You blew off the deadline." | "The doc was due Friday and was shared Tuesday." |
| "You were dismissive of Priya in standup." | "When Priya finished, you said 'we already tried that' and moved to the next item." |
| "You never follow through." | "Of the four action items from our last three 1:1s, two are still open." |
| "You're checked out." | "You've been in about half the team syncs this month." |
| "You shipped recklessly." | "The pricing change went to 100% of users without a staged rollout." |

The left column feels more honest to the person writing it, because it is what they
actually believe. It is also unarguable-with — which is exactly the problem. The right
column can be confirmed or corrected. That is what makes a conversation possible.

**Watch for these smuggled judgments:** *always, never, constantly, sloppy, careless,
lazy, unprofessional, defensive, dismissive, checked out, doesn't care, blew off,
refuses to.* Each one is a conclusion wearing an observation's clothes.

**A note on why this matters beyond politeness.** Feedback built on trait words rather
than incidents — "attitude," "communication style," "executive presence," "not a
culture fit" — is unfalsifiable, so the recipient cannot act on it or dispute it. It
is also the documented shape of biased feedback, and it lands disproportionately on
women and underrepresented employees. If a point cannot be reduced to something a
camera recorded, it is not ready to deliver. Either find the incident or drop the
point.

## Step 2 — Feelings, not accusations in disguise

Naming the feeling gives the other person the stake. Skipping it makes the message
read as a performance review; overplaying it makes it read as a complaint.

**Real feelings** describe an internal state: *frustrated, concerned, anxious,
uneasy, disappointed, discouraged, relieved, encouraged, energized, confused, tired,
uncomfortable, worried, glad, surprised.*

**Pseudo-feelings** look like feelings but are accusations with "I feel" bolted on
the front. Each one embeds a claim about what the *other* person did:

> ignored · dismissed · disrespected · let down · undermined · blindsided ·
> manipulated · unheard · unsupported · unappreciated · micromanaged · overlooked ·
> taken for granted · pressured · attacked · patronized

"I feel dismissed" is not a feeling — it is the sentence "you dismissed me" in
costume, and it will be heard that way. The move is to name the actual emotion, then
put the reason in the Need step where it belongs:

- ❌ "I felt undermined when you replied to the client directly."
- ✅ "I felt anxious when the reply went to the client before we'd aligned — I rely on
  us being consistent in front of customers."

The second version says more, blames less, and is harder to argue with.

**One is usually enough.** A single accurate feeling is stronger than three stacked
ones. In a written document, feelings should appear once or twice, not in every bullet.

## Step 3 — Needs, the part that gets skipped

Feelings come from needs — what the user values, relies on, or is responsible for.
Naming the need converts a complaint into a reason. It is also the step that most
often changes the user's own mind about what they're actually upset about.

**How to find it:** ask "why did that bother me?" and then ask it again of the answer.
Keep going until you reach something that would be true even if this person and this
incident had never existed. That is the need.

> "The doc was late." → *Why did that bother me?* → "I had to rewrite the deck at
> 11pm." → *Why did that bother me?* → "I couldn't tell the team what to expect." →
> **I rely on being able to make commitments I can keep.**

**Common needs at work:** clarity · predictability · follow-through · trust ·
autonomy · quality · fairness · being heard · shared context · focus · momentum ·
recognition · safety · efficiency · collaboration · ownership · consistency.

**Drop the word "need."** In a work setting it reads as either clinical or needy.
Natural alternatives: *"I rely on…," "I value…," "…matters to me," "…is important for
the team," "I'm responsible for…," "what I care about here is…"*

## Step 4 — Owning your part

If the user contributed to the situation, saying so early defuses most defensiveness.
It is also frequently true: unclear expectations, delayed feedback, and silent
frustration are manager-side failures.

Prompts to surface it:
- Did I actually state this expectation, or assume it was obvious?
- Is this the first time they're hearing it, or the first time I've said it out loud?
- How long have I been annoyed about this without raising it?
- Did I give them what they needed to succeed — context, time, access, a decision?
- What am I getting out of not having raised this sooner?

Examples:
- "I contributed to this by never defining what 'done' means for a launch."
- "My part in this is that I noticed it in March and am only raising it now."
- "I never told you that the Friday update is what I use to brief the leadership team."

**Own it, then move on.** This is one or two sentences, not an apology tour.
Over-apologizing before a request reads as softening the recipient up for a blow, and
it muddies whose behavior is actually under discussion.

## Step 5 — Requests, not demands

A complaint without a request leaves the other person with nothing to do except feel
bad. Requests are what make feedback actionable.

A good request is:

1. **Positive** — what to *do*, not what to stop. "Flag risks in the Monday update"
   beats "stop surprising me."
2. **Specific** — who does what, by when, observably. If you cannot tell whether it
   happened, it is not a request.
3. **Flexible** — a real question, open to negotiation. If the answer can only be yes,
   it is a demand; say so honestly rather than dressing it as a request.

**Phrasing:** *"Would you be willing to…?" · "Could it work for you to…?" · "How would
it be if…?" · "What would you think about…?"*

**Requests vs. demands at work.** Some things genuinely are not optional — a
compliance deadline, a required process. That is fine. State those as expectations,
plainly, and keep the request form for the things that are actually open. Mislabeling
a demand as a request destroys trust faster than stating the demand would have.

**When the answer is no,** get curious before getting firm:
- "What's making that hard?"
- "What would need to be true for that to work?"
- "What would you propose instead?"
- "Is this a capacity problem or a priority problem?"

## Framing the opening

How a conversation starts shapes its trajectory. Give the other person the topic and
the intent before the content, so they are not blindsided into defending themselves.

- "I'd like to talk about how the last launch went — my read and yours. Do you have
  30 minutes Thursday?"
- "I want to give you some direct feedback about follow-through. It's fixable, and I
  want to be clear rather than vague about it."
- "Something's been bothering me and I've sat on it too long, which is on me. Can we
  find time this week?"

If a written document is going out ahead of the conversation, **the document is the
frame.** Send it far enough in advance that the person can read it, react privately,
and arrive ready — a few hours to a day. Sending it minutes before is an ambush, and
sending it a week early leaves it to fester.

## Putting it together

> **Observation:** "Over the last quarter, three of the projects marked done in our
> tracker came back for rework within two weeks."
> **Feeling:** "I've been concerned about it."
> **Need:** "I rely on 'done' meaning the same thing for both of us when I'm
> committing dates to other teams."
> **My part:** "I've never written down what done actually requires, which made that
> easy to get wrong."
> **Request:** "Would you be willing to work with me on a short definition-of-done
> checklist this week, and use it before you move anything to done?"

> **Observation:** "The pricing change went out to all users at once rather than as a
> staged rollout."
> **Feeling:** "I was uneasy when I found out after the fact."
> **Need:** "For changes that touch revenue, I need to be able to tell leadership what
> the blast radius is before it's live."
> **Request:** "For anything touching billing or pricing, could it work for you to
> bring me the rollout plan before launch — even a two-line version?"

## Don't sound like NVC

This is the most common failure mode, and it matters more in writing than in speech.

The four steps are how the user *thinks through* the message. They are not a template
the reader should be able to detect. Output that marches "I observe… I feel… I need…
I request…" reads as therapeutic, condescending, and faintly manipulative — and a
direct report will recognize it as a technique being applied to them.

**Write like a confident, plain-spoken leader.** Short sentences. Specifics.
No hedging. Warmth where it is real, candor where it is needed.

| Sounds like a workshop | Sounds like a leader |
|---|---|
| "When I observe that deadlines are not met, I feel frustrated because I have a need for reliability." | "Three of the last five deliverables landed late. I need to be able to commit to dates with confidence." |
| "I'm noticing a story I'm telling myself about your engagement." | "You've been quiet in the last few syncs, and I don't want to guess why." |
| "Would you be willing to hold space for this feedback?" | "I want to talk through something directly. It's fixable." |

The rigor of the method should be invisible in the output. If the reader can name the
framework, it has been applied too literally.

---

## Scope limit

This method is for ordinary workplace friction: missed expectations, unclear
ownership, interpersonal conflict, performance patterns, and feedback between
colleagues.

**It is not appropriate for harassment, discrimination, retaliation, threats, or
safety issues.** Those are not communication problems and must not be reframed as
mutual misunderstandings — doing so puts the burden on the wrong person. When a
situation has any of those dimensions, say so plainly and route the user to HR, legal,
or an external advocate rather than helping them script a conversation.

Nor is it a substitute for therapy, counseling, or mental health care.
