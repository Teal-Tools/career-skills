# Reference: Interview Technique

Use this reference when a skill needs an adaptive "grill me" interview: career
profile intake, career clarity, performance self-review, interview prep, win-log, and
career check-ins.

The point is not to ask many questions. The point is to ask the next question that
turns a vague answer into usable evidence.

## Core rules

- **One question at a time.** Do not dump a form. Ask, wait, adapt.
- **Use the user's words.** Quote the key phrase back, then ask for specificity.
- **Dig on vague answers.** "I led the project" is not enough. Ask what they did,
  what broke, who was involved, and what changed.
- **Separate fact from interpretation.** Especially for reviews, check-ins, conflict,
  and career clarity: feelings are data, not proof.
- **Quantify whenever possible.** Ask for scope, before/after, count, dollars,
  percent, time saved, quality change, risk reduced, audience size, team size, or
  frequency.
- **Follow energy.** Notice where the user becomes sharper, more specific, evasive, or
  flat. That is signal for career direction and sustainability.
- **Stop when the deliverable is well-supported.** Do not interrogate for completeness
  after the output is already clear.

## Question ladder

Use this ladder repeatedly. Skip steps that are already answered by the source
material.

1. **Surface:** What happened?
2. **Specificity:** What did you personally do?
3. **Context:** Why did it matter then? What was hard about it?
4. **Scope:** Who or what was affected? How many users, dollars, people, systems,
   candidates, customers, teams, or regions?
5. **Outcome:** What changed afterward?
6. **Evidence:** What proves it? Link, review note, metric, shipped artifact,
   customer quote, manager feedback, PR, dashboard, calendar event, or witness.
7. **Counter-evidence:** What points the other way?
8. **Meaning:** What does this imply for the user's next decision or deliverable?

Good follow-ups:
- "What did you do that nobody else did?"
- "What was the before state?"
- "How would someone else know this worked?"
- "What number would make this less hand-wavy?"
- "Who noticed?"
- "What would your manager say was the impact?"
- "What are you inferring rather than observing?"
- "What would change your mind?"

## Evidence confidence

When a skill depends on judgment, classify claims before acting on them.

- **Strong evidence:** direct feedback, documented outcomes, repeated signals, clear
  metrics, explicit decisions, or multiple independent examples.
- **Mixed evidence:** real signals exist, but there is meaningful counter-evidence or
  the pattern is inconsistent.
- **Weak signal:** one anecdote, ambiguous silence, a vibe, or an unverified third-hand
  read.
- **Story:** a plausible interpretation with little or no evidence yet.
- **Unknown:** the question matters, but the user needs more data.

Use the classification in the deliverable. It keeps the agent from turning anxiety,
confidence, or politeness into false certainty.

## Tone

Be direct and warm. The voice is "honest colleague helping you get clearer," not
therapist, judge, or hype person.

Do:
- validate that the user's experience matters;
- challenge unsupported certainty;
- name patterns carefully;
- ask for proof without shaming the user for not having it;
- preserve user agency.

Do not:
- diagnose the user or other people;
- tell the user what their employer secretly believes;
- inflate wins to sound impressive;
- accept self-minimizing answers when evidence suggests real impact;
- push dramatic decisions when a small evidence-gathering action is enough.

## Branch patterns

### Capability dump / career-profile
Goal: recover the real history behind thin resumes and LinkedIn profiles.

Ask by role:
- "What were you responsible for that is not obvious from the title?"
- "What were the 2-3 things people came to you for?"
- "What was the hardest problem you solved there?"
- "What did you ship, fix, grow, reduce, or prevent?"
- "What numbers could prove the scope or result?"

Stop when the role has dates, scope, headline wins, and any open `to-confirm` items.

### Win-log
Goal: turn recent activity into evidence-backed wins.

Ask:
- "What got shipped, decided, unblocked, improved, or prevented?"
- "Who benefited?"
- "What evidence can we link to?"
- "What metric is missing, and who would know it?"

Stop when each confirmed win has what happened, impact, evidence, and a resume-ready
bullet or a clear metric TODO.

### Career-checkin / self-review
Goal: produce an honest read of performance, perception, and alignment.

Ask:
- "What do you believe is true?"
- "What proves that?"
- "What points the other way?"
- "What would we need to know before treating this as fact?"
- "What is the smallest next move that would create clarity?"

Stop when there is enough evidence to classify confidence and choose 1-3 next actions.

### Career-clarity
Goal: distinguish running toward from running away.

Ask:
- "What parts of work give you energy even when they are hard?"
- "What drains you even when it goes well?"
- "If the current irritant disappeared, would you still want a change?"
- "What future are you willing to practice for?"
- "What small experiment would test that path without overcommitting?"
- "Do you want more zero-to-one creation, one-to-ten scaling, or later-stage
  optimization?"
- "What environment makes you sharper: fast-paced ambiguity, a mature operating
  system, deep craft time, high-collaboration influence work, customer-facing
  urgency, or something else?"
- "Which skills are you good at but tired of using?"
- "Which domains or problems make you curious before anyone pays you to care?"

Stop when the user has energy patterns, values/workstyle hypotheses, transferable
skills, candidate directions, and experiments.

### Interview-prep
Goal: build credible stories, not generic answers.

Ask:
- "Which story best proves this competency?"
- "What was the situation, task, action, result, and lesson?"
- "Where might an interviewer poke holes?"
- "What version is short enough for a live interview?"

Stop when the story bank covers the likely competencies with evidence-backed STAR
answers.
